# AI Critique

The AI was useful for brainstorming and for turning messy notes into a structured draft, but it was unreliable whenever the task depended on the exact wording of the requirements. In this assignment, it tended to generalize from common report templates instead of reading the rubric closely: it could produce polished sections, yet it missed clarifications such as which artifacts were mandatory, which screenshots had to show my username, and which evidence had to be my own instead of AI-generated. It also showed a subtle bias toward overconfident completion, presenting incomplete answers as if they were final and sometimes inventing extra constraints or dismissing real ones as hallucinations. That happened because the model is optimized to produce plausible text quickly, not to verify every clause against the source document or to notice when a requirement is unusually specific. The AI also lacks the real-world context needed for this HW, especially when judging whether a physical-device test case is genuinely an edge case or whether a defect description is actually grounded in evidence. The main principle I learned is to use AI as a first-pass assistant, never as an authority: I should feed it the exact requirement, then verify every claim against the assignment text, my own observations, and the required rubric before accepting anything.
